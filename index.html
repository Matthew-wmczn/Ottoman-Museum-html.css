The Ottoman Empire (1299–1922) was one of history’s longest-lasting and most influential states, spanning over six centuries and three continents. Rising from a small frontier principality in Anatolia, the Ottomans built a vast, multi-ethnic empire that stretched from the gates of Vienna to the deserts of Arabia, and from North Africa to the Caucasus. Its power rested on a unique blend of political authority, military innovation, and cultural diversity. The empire’s capital, Istanbul—once Constantinople—became a vibrant center of trade, art, religion, and diplomacy, linking East and West. Ottoman society embraced a complex mix of traditions: Islamic law shaped governance, while Christian and Jewish communities lived under the millet system; monumental architecture such as the Blue Mosque and Topkapı Palace showcased artistic mastery; and daily life thrived in markets, coffeehouses, and caravanserais. Over centuries, the empire faced both triumph and challenge, adapting to shifting technologies and political realities, until its eventual dissolution after World War I, paving the way for the Republic of Turkey.
